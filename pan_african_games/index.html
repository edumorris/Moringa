Ajua/Bao

commonly known as board game is a men only game among the Luo. The game is played on a board bearing sixteen holes; eight each side. Though also common among most communities in Kenya, the number of holes and rules of the game varies from one place and the other. He game is usually played by two people at a time in which the winner gets a price in the form of livestock. At the onset, each player organizes his side of the board as he sees fit. Every time a player wins some chips, he puts them aside until it is no longer possible to play. At the end, the player rearranges the board as they found it before the game, and it is usually at this point that the winner is determined. The significance of the game is not only to pass time but also a measure of accuracy, speed and wittiness.

Peleka Barua

is a Swahili traditional game that involves a number of participants in the range of 2-20 or more. This sit down in a circular manner with one person identified to lead in a song while walking around the circle. The chosen one suddenly touches anyone in the circle to run round going anticlockwise. He/she can do a number of rounds before touching the next victim to do the same rounds as he/she comes to take his/her original position and the game continues in that manner. The game is popular is between age4-13yrs of age.

Instructions to Play Ajua

  1. Pebbles, stones or marbles are placed in the holes.
  2. Each hole may contain 3 to 5 marbles
  3. The players re-arrange the seeds in their row. Holes can become empty, but there must be at least one hole whose contents can reach an occupied hole on the opponent's side on the first lap, when sown clockwise.
  4. On his turn, a player must distribute the contents of the leftmost hole of his row in a clockwise direction, which reaches an occupied hole of the opponent's side in the first lap. If this is not possible, a player may sow the contents of the leftmost hole, which ends in the first lap in an opponent's hole that is empty. If this is not possible either, he must sow the contents of the leftmost hole, which ends in an empty hole on his side.
  5. When the last seed falls into an occupied hole, which hasn't been "married", its contents are picked up and distributed in another lap.
  6. If the last seed is dropped into an empty hole, an opponent's hole containing just one seed (after either player has captured) or a "married" hole, the move ends ("sleeps").

Instructions to Play Peleka Barau

  1. Children, or some cases adults, sit in a circle, and a player is selected.
  2. The player then runs around the circle to the beat of a song.
  3. Once the song stops, the sitting player that the running player has stopped at is it.
  4. Both players run in opposite directions, and try to get to the open sitting space first.
  5. The player that gets to the open sitting space first wins.

Instructions to Play Draft

  1. With an 8x8 board, "playing caps" are placed on a set of boxes with same color.
  2. Each player gets 12 playing caps to begin with. Each players cap or of a different color or different design for easier identification.
  3. The goal of the game is to capture all of the opponents caps.
  4. In most cases, the player with the darker caps makes the first move. In other cases, the players flip for the first move.
  5. To capture an opponents caps, the player has to "jump over" diagonally over the other opponents pieces.
  6. In the cases that a piece reaches the opposite end of the board, the cap is crowned. Crowned caps have more more in that they can move both foward and backward.
